## Why Minimum Deposit Matters (And When It Doesn't)

### What Minimum Deposit Actually Tells You

The minimum deposit is the smallest amount a broker requires to open a live trading account. It reflects the broker's target market and accessibility philosophy — but it says very little about trading quality.

Some brokers with $0 minimums (like Pepperstone) offer world-class ECN execution. Others with $200 minimums (like IC Markets) justify the threshold with deeper liquidity and premium infrastructure. Neither approach is inherently better.

### When Low Minimum Deposit Matters Most

- **Beginners** testing live markets with real money for the first time

- **Budget-conscious traders** in developing markets where $200 is a significant amount

## How to Trade Safely with a Small Account

### Position Sizing Is Everything

With a $100 account and 30:1 leverage, you can theoretically open a position worth $3,000. But if EUR/USD moves 33 pips against you, your entire account is gone. Smart position sizing keeps you alive:

- **Risk no more than 1-2% per trade** — on a $100 account, that's $1-$2 maximum risk
- **Use micro-lots (0.01)** — each pip is worth approximately $0.10
- **Set stop-losses on every trade** — no exceptions
- **Don't over-leverage** — just because you can use 30:1 doesn't mean you should

### Focus on Major Pairs

Major pairs (EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY) have the tightest spreads and highest liquidity. With a small account, spread costs matter proportionally more. Trading exotic pairs with 3-5 pip spreads will destroy a small account through transaction costs alone.

### Demo First, Then Micro Live

Before risking real money, practice on a demo account for at least 2-3 months. When you transition to live, start with the minimum deposit and trade micro-lots. Prove you can be profitable on a small account before adding more capital.

### Avoid These Common Small-Account Mistakes

1. **Over-leveraging** — using maximum leverage on every trade
2. **Revenge trading** — increasing position size after a loss to "make it back"
3. **Ignoring spreads** — trading high-spread pairs that eat your profit margin
4. **No stop-loss** — hoping the market will turn in your favor
5. **Chasing news events** — volatility spikes can blow small accounts instantly
