## Why Regulation Matters More Than Anything Else

### What Regulation Actually Protects

Regulated brokers must comply with rules that protect your money and ensure fair trading:

1. **Fund Segregation** — Your money must be held in separate bank accounts from the broker's operating funds. If the broker goes bankrupt, your money is protected.

2. **Capital Adequacy** — Regulated brokers must maintain minimum capital reserves (€730,000 for CySEC, £1 million for FCA). This ensures they can weather market shocks without using client funds.

3. **Negative Balance Protection** — You can't lose more than your account balance. If a market crash causes your account to go negative, the broker absorbs the loss.

4. **Regular Audits** — Regulated brokers undergo regular financial audits by independent firms and their regulator.

5. **Compensation Schemes** — Many regulators operate compensation funds that protect clients if a broker fails. FSCS (UK) covers up to £85,000 per client.

6. **Transparent Pricing** — Regulated brokers must provide fair and transparent pricing without artificial manipulation.

### What Happens With Unregulated Brokers

Unregulated or poorly regulated brokers can:

- **Comingle funds** — use your money for their operating expenses
- **Manipulate prices** — widen spreads artificially to trigger stop losses
- **Refuse withdrawals** — delay or deny your withdrawal requests with no recourse
- **Go bankrupt** — with no capital requirements, insolvency is always a risk
- **Offer excessive leverage** — while it sounds attractive, 1000:1 leverage dramatically increases your risk of total loss

Not all regulators are equal. We classify them into three tiers based on their enforcement power, capital requirements, and client protection measures.

|-----------|---------|-----------------|--------------|

## How to Verify a Broker's Regulation

### Step 1: Check the Regulator's Official Register

Every Tier-1 regulator maintains a public register of licensed firms:

- **FCA:** [register.fca.org.uk](https://register.fca.org.uk)
- **ASIC:** [asic.gov.au/online-services/search-asics-registers](https://asic.gov.au/online-services/search-asics-registers)
- **MAS:** [eservices.mas.gov.sg/fid](https://eservices.mas.gov.sg/fid)
- **CFTC/NFA:** [nfa.futures.org/basicnet](https://nfa.futures.org/basicnet)

### Step 2: Verify the License Number

Cross-reference the license number on the broker's website with the regulator's register. The company name, address, and license number must match exactly.

### Step 3: Check for Warnings

Search the regulator's website for any enforcement actions, warnings, or fines against the broker. A clean record is essential.

### Step 4: Confirm Client Protections

Verify that the specific protections (fund segregation, negative balance protection, compensation scheme) apply to the entity that will hold your account. Some brokers have multiple entities — the protections depend on which entity you're onboarded to.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the most regulated forex broker?

**IG** is the most regulated forex broker in the world, with licenses from 8+ Tier-1 regulators including the FCA, ASIC, MAS, BaFin, CFTC, DFSA, FMA, and JFSA. It's also a FTSE 250 listed company, providing full financial transparency.

### Are regulated forex brokers safe?

Regulated brokers are significantly safer than unregulated ones, but "safe" is relative. Fund segregation, negative balance protection, and compensation schemes reduce your risk but don't eliminate it. Always choose Tier-1 regulated brokers and diversify across multiple brokers if your account balance exceeds compensation scheme limits.

### Can I trade with an unregulated broker?

You can, but we strongly advise against it. Unregulated brokers are not subject to fund segregation requirements, capital adequacy rules, or regular audits. If they go bankrupt or engage in fraud, you have no legal recourse and no compensation scheme to fall back on.

### Which regulator is the strictest?

The **CFTC/NFA** (USA) is the strictest regulator, requiring $20 million+ in minimum capital, 50:1 leverage limits on major pairs, and extensive reporting requirements. The **FCA** (UK) and **ASIC** (Australia) are close seconds. These three are considered the gold standard of forex regulation.

### What is negative balance protection?

Negative balance protection ensures you can never lose more than your account balance. If a market crash or gap causes your positions to go into negative territory, the broker absorbs the loss. This is mandatory under FCA and CySEC regulation but not universally required elsewhere.
